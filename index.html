I am Amelia Fraser-McKelvie, a postdoctoral researcher in galaxy evolution at the University of Nottingham, formerley PhD student at Monash University and the University of Hull. At Nottingham I am working on the MaNGA Galaxy survey, an integral field spectroscopy survey aiming to provide spatially-resolved spectroscopy of 10,000 galaxies by 2020.

My latest paper is on Lenticular galaxies in MaNGA.. It seems there are two separate populations of S0s, separated chiefly by stellar mass. These populations differ in their stellar age and metallicity, from which we imply separate formation mechanisms. The separation of S0 galaxies into their individual bulge and disk components makes this analysis possible, and I am interested in what else we can learn about galaxy evolution by breaking galaxies down into their separate components.

I am currently working with Prof Alfonso Arag√≥n-Salamanca & Prof Mike Merrifield, along with the rest of the MaNGA team, investigating the formation and evolution of various galaxy structures. In particular, I am interested in what the stellar populations of bulges, disks, and bars in galaxies can tell us about their formation history and mechanisms driving their evolution.

At Monash and Hull I worked with Dr. Kevin Pimbblet and Prof Michael Brown on populations of galaxies that lie outside of our expected correlations between observable properties. I studied local brightest cluster galaxies, massive isolated void galaxies, and passive spiral galaxies using both precision photometry and integral field spectroscopy.
